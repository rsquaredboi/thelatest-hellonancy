So what is the Lem, exactly?

The Lem is a clitoral suction toy — part of a category of vibes that, instead of buzzing against you, uses air pulses to create a kind of gentle vacuum seal. The design brief was apparently “what if the sex toy didn’t look like a sex toy at all,” and the result is a small, matte-yellow, lemon-shaped silicone object that absolutely could live on your nightstand next to a candle and not raise a single eyebrow.

It’s tiny — about the size of a lemon, which tracks — with a single button, a soft nozzle at one end, and 12 intensity settings. There’s no app. No Bluetooth. No subscription. It’s one of maybe three sex toys I’ve tested this year that doesn’t want to be a platform.

How it actually works

The short version: you press and hold the button to turn it on, tap it to cycle through intensities, and place the nozzle over your clit. That’s the entire user manual. If you can operate a button, you can operate the Lem.

  1. Charge it first. A full charge takes about 90 minutes and gets you two hours of playtime, which is either generous or deeply ambitious, depending on your weekend plans.
  2. Test-drive the settings on your palm. The jump from level 5 to level 6 is noticeable. You do not want to discover this mid-session.
  3. Add water-based lube. Not silicone lube — it ruins silicone toys. Oil-based stains sheets. Water-based is the answer.
  4. Start at level 1 and work your way up. This toy has more headroom than you think. Level 12 is not a polite suggestion.
  5. Take it in the shower if you want. IPX7 means it’s fine under water. Nancy’s return page confirms this, with enthusiasm.
TIP Warm the nozzle in your hand for ten seconds before you use it. Cold silicone on sensitive skin is a mood-killer, and the Lem’s matte finish holds body heat surprisingly well once it’s warmed up.

I spent three weeks with it. Here’s what happened.

Week one was exploratory. I charged it, I squinted at it suspiciously, I tested it on the back of my hand, and I immediately understood why people were losing their minds in comment sections. The sensation is weird at first — it’s not buzzing, it’s not pulsing, it’s more like a very insistent, very targeted little kiss. On level 2 it’s sleepy. On level 5 it’s focused. On level 9 it’s a problem.

Week two I got greedy. I started testing it in contexts other reviewers had mentioned — in the bath, during a show (don’t worry, I paused), as a warm-up before partnered sex. The water test was the most impressive: most “waterproof” toys are technically waterproof but you still feel weirdly anxious about submerging them. The Lem I stopped thinking about. It just works.

Week three I tried to break it. I charged it in the humid bathroom. I dropped it (onto a bath mat, don’t panic). I used it back-to-back-to-back in one afternoon to test the battery claims. It handled all of it. The only complaint I can muster after three weeks is the single-button control cycle: if you overshoot and need to go back down, you have to click all the way through level 12 to reset. It’s a small thing. You will notice it once and then adapt.

How it stacks up against the big names

Against the Womanizer Premium 2: the Lem is a third of the price and about 80% as powerful. For most people, that’s a trade they’ll take every time. The Womanizer has two buttons (up and down), which is a genuine advantage, but it’s also almost twice the size and feels like tech — the Lem feels like an object.

Against the Lelo Sona 2: the Lem is quieter and cheaper, but the Sona has sonic wave tech that goes deeper. If you like really intense, you might prefer the Sona. If you like targeted and teasing, the Lem wins.

Against the Rose: the Lem is not an “iconic shape” — but it’s also not a dropshipped Aliexpress knockoff, which most Roses are at this point. You’re paying for a real product with real engineering, not a viral aesthetic.
